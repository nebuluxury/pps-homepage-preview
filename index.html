Custom vs. Factory Strings & Value

What's the real difference between custom and factory strings?

Honestly, a good factory string and a good custom string are made from similar materials. The difference is the build: a custom string is made for your bow, pre-stretched under heavy tension, served the way you want, and built one at a time instead of mass-produced. The practical wins are consistency (less creep and peep rotation once it's settled), the exact colors you want, and being able to talk to the person who built it. We won't tell you a factory string is junk — we'll tell you a custom string is dialed in.

My bow shoots fine. Why would I replace the string?

Two reasons: wear and personalization. Strings are a wear item — the serving frays, the string fuzzes, and performance drifts over time. If yours is a few years old, worn, or weathered, fresh strings restore consistency. The other reason is simple: you want your colors and a string built specifically for you.

Are custom strings actually worth it for an average shooter?

We'll be straight with you. Factory strings on newer bows are much better than they used to be, so a casual backyard shooter may not see a night-and-day difference. The value shows up most for people who shoot a lot, hunt hard, or want a rock-solid hold on tune — and for anyone who wants exact colors and a real person standing behind the product.

Will custom strings make my bow faster?

A string alone won't magically add a bunch of FPS. What a quality, properly pre-stretched string does is keep your speed and tune consistent shot to shot, recover speed lost from an old fuzzed-out string, and let speed nocks be tuned in for a little more. The real gains are consistency and reliability, not headline speed numbers.

Will a custom string make me more accurate?

The string itself doesn't add accuracy — but a stable string that doesn't creep or rotate keeps your bow in tune shot after shot, which lets you shoot to your own potential consistently. The gain is repeatability: your tune doesn't drift on you.

Materials & Strand Count

What material are your strings made from?

Confirm fibers stocked We build with premium bowstring fibers — typically BCY 452X, X99, and Mercury — chosen for low creep and stability. The right one depends on your setup and how you shoot; tell us your bow and we'll recommend the best fit.

What is BCY 452X and why is it so popular?

452X is a blend of roughly two-thirds SK99 Dyneema and one-third Vectran. The Vectran makes it extremely stable with essentially no creep after break-in, which is why it's the most popular all-around hunting and target material. The only tradeoff is it can fuzz a little more than pure-Dyneema materials.

How is X99 different from 452X?

X99 has more Dyneema and less Vectran than 452X. The result is a slightly faster string that fuzzes less and shows brighter colors, at the cost of a tiny bit more potential creep. A lot of shooters love X99 for the clean look and low maintenance.

What is Mercury and should I pick it?

Mercury is a newer material known for a soft, quiet shot — often even quieter than X99. It has no Vectran, so it's slightly more prone to stretch than 452X. It's a great pick if a quiet, smooth shot is your top priority.

452X vs. X99 vs. Mercury — simple way to choose?

Pick 452X for maximum stability if you don't mind a little fuzz; pick X99 for less fuzz, bright colors, and a touch more speed; pick Mercury for the quietest, softest shot. For most hunters, 452X or X99 are the safe choices — and we're happy to recommend one for your bow.

What does "creep-resistant" actually mean?

Creep is permanent lengthening of the string over time. The Vectran in blends like 452X and X99 essentially eliminates creep at normal bow tensions. Pure Dyneema materials are a hair faster but creep more — which is why quality compound strings use Vectran blends.

How many strands should my string have?

Strand count is matched to the material, your cam-groove width, and nock fit — common compound counts run in the high-teens to low-20s. Fewer strands can be slightly faster, but go below the recommended count and the string loses mass, giving you more noise and vibration after the shot. We build to the right count for your setup.

Does a thinner string with fewer strands make my bow faster?

Slightly — less mass on the string can pick up a few FPS, but the gain is small and there's a floor. Go too thin and you trade away durability, add vibration, and risk poor nock fit. We stay within the material's recommended range for a reason.

What is serving, and what do you use?

Serving is the thread wrapped over the string at the loops, cam grooves, and nock point to protect the fibers and set nock fit. Confirm default servings We use quality center and end servings (such as BCY Powergrip or Halo) chosen for durability and separation resistance.

Measuring & Ordering

How do I order custom strings?

Send your bow make, model, and year plus draw weight, draw length, and your color choices — through the contact form or the builder above. We'll confirm the exact specs with you, then build to match.

What's the most reliable way to get the right length?

For a compound, give your exact bow make, model, and year — that lets us build to the manufacturer's published spec. On compounds, the model is more reliable than a tape measurement of an old, possibly-crept string.

Where do I find my bow's string and cable specs?

They're usually on a sticker on the limb, in your owner's manual, or on the bow maker's website. Can't find them? Tell us the model and we'll look them up, or your pro shop can.

How do I measure my existing string or cable myself?

Strings and cables are measured under 100 lbs of tension: put a 1/4-inch post through each end loop, stretch to 100 lbs, and measure post-to-post (outside to outside). Just know that copying an old string only works if your bow's brace height and timing are already where you want them.

My measured length doesn't match the factory spec — which do I use?

Factory spec is the safest reference, because an old string may have crept or been twisted off-spec over time. If your bow is tuned exactly how you like it and you want a duplicate, measuring works — just tell us so we build to the right target.

What if my bow is old or discontinued?

Still doable. Older bows just need a little more info — usually a measurement of your existing string and cables. Send us what you've got and we'll figure it out together.

Should I send you my old string to copy?

Decide your preference It can help for an unknown or modified setup, but remember a worn or crept string can give a misleading length. Often your bow model and specs are all we need — ask us and we'll tell you.

Do you build for single-cam and dual-cam bows?

Yes — complete sets, single strings, and single cables for single-cam, hybrid-cam, and dual-cam compounds. Just tell us your setup.

Can I order just one string or one cable?

Yes. Single strings and single cables are available individually if you only need to replace one piece — though pairing a fresh string with worn cables can affect timing (see the install section).

Colors & Customization

How many colors can I choose?

You can run a single solid color, a two-color twist, or add a contrasting serving color — plus options like fades and camo. Confirm color/pattern menu Pick what matches your bow or go bold; it's your build.

Can the serving be a different color than the string?

Yes — serving comes in its own range of colors, and the center serving and end servings can differ from the string body. It's an easy way to add a pop of contrast.

Why does a light serving look like a different color?

Light and fluorescent servings are slightly translucent, so the string color underneath shows through — for example, flo-yellow serving over a blue string reads as green. We'll steer you toward color pairings that come out clean.

Can you match my bow's camo or a specific color?

We'll get as close as the available fibers allow. Tell us what you're matching and we'll recommend the best combination.

What are speed nocks and do I need them?

Speed nocks are small weighted bands added at specific spots on the string to dampen oscillation and tune harmonics, placed per your bow's recommendation. Whether you need them depends on your bow — we'll let you know.

Will my colors fade over time?

Quality fibers hold color well with normal use. Long-term UV and weather will dull any string somewhat — storing it out of direct sun and keeping it waxed helps. We won't promise a specific fade timeline because honestly it depends on your conditions.

Performance: Creep, Stretch & Peep Rotation

What does "pre-stretched" mean and why does it matter?

After the string is built, it's put under heavy tension to remove most of the initial settling before it ever goes on your bow. A properly pre-stretched string settles in within a handful of shots and then holds its length — that's what keeps your peep, draw length, and tune from drifting after a week of shooting.

What's the difference between stretch and creep?

Stretch is temporary and recoverable — every string stretches during the shot as energy transfers through it, then returns to length. Creep is permanent lengthening that doesn't come back, and it's what shifts your draw length, peep position, and tune. Quality Vectran-blend materials essentially eliminate creep after break-in.

Why does my peep sight rotate?

Mostly from the string settling and twisting during break-in, and from creep if a lower-grade material keeps elongating. A little rotation in the first shots on a brand-new string is normal; persistent rotation after break-in usually means creep or a string that wasn't twisted and pre-stretched properly.

My peep rotated after install — is the string defective?

Not necessarily. A new string settles for roughly the first hundred shots, and minor peep movement in that window is normal — it's corrected with a slight re-twist. Persistent rotation after break-in, on a creep-resistant material, is what would point to a real problem.

Do the twists in a string actually matter?

Yes. Twists hold the strands together, fine-tune the string's length, help it settle, and reduce peep rotation. There's a healthy range (roughly half to three-quarters of a twist per inch); too few or too many both cause issues, which is part of why a hand-built string is built carefully.

Installation

Do I need a bow press to install a string?

For a compound, yes — a bow press safely compresses the limbs so the string and cables can be swapped. Without one you can't safely change a compound string, which is why a lot of folks have a pro shop do it.

Should I install it myself or have a shop do it?

If you have a press and the experience, it's a straightforward job. If you're unsure, have a pro shop install and tune it — improper installation can damage the string and voids most warranties. Note if you offer install/tuning

How do I keep the string from losing its twists during install?

We ship strings pre-twisted to spec, usually secured (a paper clip works) so the set twist isn't lost. Keep it secured until the string is on the bow and under tension, then make any fine adjustments afterward.

What is cam timing and why does it matter after a string change?

On dual-cam and hybrid-cam bows, the cams must roll over together at full draw; if a new string or cables shift that, the bow shoots inconsistently. Timing is checked at full draw and corrected by adding or removing half-twists from a cable, one at a time.

Can I replace just the string and leave my old cables?

You can, but mixing a fresh string with worn cables (or vice versa) can throw off timing and tune, and the old parts may be near end of life. For consistency, many shooters replace the full set together — but we sell single pieces if that's what you need.

Tuning After Install

Do I need to re-tune my bow after a new string?

Yes — even a perfectly built string means checking cam timing, nock point, peep position, and doing a paper or walk-back tune to confirm arrow flight. Plan on re-tuning as part of any string change.

How many shots until a new string settles in?

A common recommendation is about 100 arrows to let it finish settling before final tuning, so any minor initial movement is out of the way. After that, re-check timing and peep alignment.

When should I install my peep sight?

Install the peep before tying the final nock point and doing fine timing, since the peep takes up space and slightly shortens the string. Make peep height changes with little or no tension on the string to avoid damaging strands.

How do I fix peep rotation after break-in?

Minor rotation is corrected by adding or removing twists (in a press, tension off) until the peep faces you at full draw. If it keeps creeping back after proper break-in on a stable material, that points to a string issue — reach out.

What is paper tuning?

Shooting an arrow through a sheet of paper from about 5–7 feet and reading the tear. The goal is a clean "bullet hole" the diameter of the arrow, which means good arrow flight; the direction of a tear tells you which way to adjust your rest or nock point.

Can I change my draw length by twisting the string?

Only small changes — draw length on a compound is mainly set by the cam or module, not the string. Adding or removing twists changes brace height and timing, so any real draw-length change should be done at the cam and then re-tuned.

Care & Maintenance

How do I wax my string, and how often?

Work bowstring wax into the bare fibers with your fingers, then rub it in with a piece of leather or cloth so the friction melts it into the strands. Roughly every couple of weeks of regular shooting, or any time the string feels dry or starts to fuzz.

Where should I NOT put wax?

Never wax the served sections — the center serving at the nock point and the serving in the cam grooves. Wax on the nock serving makes it slick so your nocks seat inconsistently, which hurts accuracy.

What kind of wax should I use?

Use a purpose-made bowstring wax (beeswax- or silicone-based, like BCY, Bohning Tex-Tite, etc.). Do not use Vaseline, petroleum jelly, or lip balm — petroleum products attract grit and can break down synthetic fibers over time.

How should I store my bow to protect the string?

Out of direct sunlight, away from heat, in a dry place. UV, heat, and moisture all shorten string life. A waxed string in a case, kept out of a hot vehicle, lasts longest.

Will leaving my bow in a hot truck hurt the string?

Yes — prolonged heat softens the wax, dries the fibers, and stresses the string. A hot car or trunk is one of the worst places to leave a bow. Keep it cool and dry.

How do I tell normal fuzz from real wear?

A little surface fuzz is normal and managed by waxing. The warning signs are heavy or persistent fuzzing, broken or cut strands, discoloration, a dry/brittle feel, and any gaps or separation in the serving — those mean it's time to replace.

Longevity & Warranty

How long does a bowstring last?

A well-maintained, properly stored set typically lasts about 2–3 years, or several thousand shots — but it varies with how much you shoot, draw weight, climate, and care. Heavy shooting or rough conditions shorten that.

How often should I replace my strings?

A common guideline is about once a year for active shooters, or sooner if you see fraying, fuzzing, serving separation, lost speed, or sudden peep rotation. We can set up a reminder so you're not guessing.

What does your warranty cover?

Confirm final warranty We stand behind our build against peep rotation, serving separation, creep, and craftsmanship defects. If something isn't right, we make it right.

What would void the warranty?

Confirm exclusions Typical exclusions are improper installation, damage from a burred or badly installed peep, neglect or lack of maintenance, modification, and abuse. Normal wear from shooting and weather isn't a defect.

Is the normal stretch during a shot a defect?

No — every string stretches (recoverable) during the shot as energy transfers from the limbs. That's normal physics. Warranties cover creep (permanent elongation), not normal shot stretch.

Crossbow Strings

Do you make crossbow strings?

Confirm availability Crossbow strings and cables are an expansion area for us. Tell us your crossbow model and we'll let you know current availability and what specs we need.

How are crossbow strings different from vertical-bow strings?

They run at far higher tension, and the center serving rides on the rail and through the trigger box, so the serving and material spec are model-specific and critical. Because of the loads involved, crossbow string work and safety matter even more.

How often should I replace a crossbow string?

Plan on roughly two seasons of regular shooting — sooner for bowfishing or wet, dirty use. Any broken strand, slipped serving, or chronic fuzzing means replace it right away, even if it's only a few months old.

What does "never dry-fire" mean, and why is it so serious?

Dry-firing is releasing the bow or crossbow with no bolt/arrow loaded. With nothing to absorb the energy, it can violently damage the limbs, string, and trigger — and injure you. Never pull the trigger without a bolt seated.

What is rail lube and how often do I apply it?

Rail lube reduces friction between the string serving and the crossbow rail, protecting the center serving. Apply it per your crossbow maker's guidance — often every 10–15 shots on some rails, or every 50–100 on others, and after any rain or heavy moisture.

Do I wax a crossbow string the same way?

Wax the exposed fibers like a vertical bow, but keep heavy wax off the center serving that rides the rail — buildup there collects grit and can gum up the trigger box. Use rail lube on the rail-contact area instead.
